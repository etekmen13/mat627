\section{Discussion}
For Parts 1 and 2, the integrand is
\[
f(x)=x^2e^{-x},
\qquad
f''(x)=(x^2-4x+2)e^{-x},
\]
so $f''$ is continuous and bounded on both closed intervals. Therefore Theorem 2.3 guarantees at least second-order convergence.

In Part 1, the observed rate approaches $2$, which matches the theorem directly.

In Part 2, the observed rate is closer to $4$.  This means the $O(h^2)$ term vanished. Indeed,
\[
f'(x)=(2x-x^2)e^{-x},
\]
so
\[
f'(0)=f'(2)=0.
\]
The leading trapezoid-rule error term is proportional to $f'(b)-f'(a)$, so on $[0,2]$ it cancels and the next nonzero term gives fourth-order behavior.

For Part 3, the integrand is
\[
f(x)=\sqrt{x},
\qquad
f''(x)=-\frac{1}{4x^{3/2}},
\]
which is singular at $x=0$. Theorem 2.3 does not apply on $[0,1]$. On the first subinterval $[0,h]$, the trapezoid rule uses the line through $(0,0)$ and $(h,\sqrt{h})$, so
\[
T_{[0,h]} = \frac{h}{2}\sqrt{h} = \frac{1}{2}h^{3/2},
\qquad
\int_0^h \sqrt{x}\,dx = \frac{2}{3}h^{3/2}.
\]
Hence the first cell alone contributes an error of
\[
\frac{2}{3}h^{3/2}-\frac{1}{2}h^{3/2} = \frac{1}{6}h^{3/2},
\]
which explains why the overall convergence rate is approximately $3/2$ rather than $2$.
